The Foundation

Why EPA Is Better Than Yards

Traditional NFL statistics — yards, completion percentage, touchdowns — are context-blind. A 5-yard gain on 3rd-and-4 is a success. A 5-yard gain on 3rd-and-10 is a failure. They look identical in the box score.

Expected Points Added (EPA) solves this. Developed from decades of NFL play-by-play data, EPA measures the change in expected points a team gains or loses on every single play. A team's rolling EPA per play is one of the most predictive statistics in football — far better than raw yards or even scoring alone.

NFL Oracle uses EPA data from nflfastR, the most comprehensive open-source NFL analytics dataset, covering every play from 2022 through the current season.

⚡
Offensive EPA
Measures how much value a team's offense adds per play. Teams with consistently positive offensive EPA win more games regardless of their record.
🛡
Defensive EPA Allowed
The EPA a defense gives up per play. Elite defenses post negative EPA allowed — they take points off the board rather than adding them.
✈️
Pass vs. Rush EPA
Passing EPA and rushing EPA are tracked separately, since teams vary dramatically in how they generate offensive value.
Team Ratings

The Elo System

NFL Oracle uses a FiveThirtyEight-style Elo rating system to track team strength over time. Originally developed for chess, Elo is now widely used in sports analytics because it handles head-to-head competition naturally.

Every team starts at 1500. After each game, Elo shifts based on two factors: the expected outcome (based on pre-game Elo difference) and the actual outcome. Beating a stronger team earns more Elo. Losing to a weaker team costs more.

Between seasons, Elo ratings regress 55% toward the mean of 1500 — accounting for roster turnover, coaching changes, and regression to the mean. A dynasty team from 3 years ago doesn't get to carry that Elo into the present season.

The Model

How Predictions Are Generated

Every prediction runs through a 6-step pipeline that processes raw play-by-play data into a calibrated win probability.

01

Data Download

188,000+ plays from the last 3 NFL seasons are downloaded from nflfastR. Only regular season games are used — playoffs are excluded because small-sample playoff performance introduces noise.

02

Feature Engineering

For each game, 43 features are computed from both teams' rolling statistics: offensive EPA, defensive EPA allowed, pass EPA, rush EPA, points scored/allowed, win rate, current streak, rest days, Elo ratings, and home field advantage.

03

Walk-Forward Cross-Validation

The model is validated using walk-forward CV — it's trained on early seasons and tested on later ones, exactly how it would perform in production. This prevents data leakage and gives honest accuracy estimates.

04

Gradient Boosting Classifier

A sklearn GradientBoostingClassifier with max_depth=3 and min_samples_leaf=15 is trained on all available data. Shallow trees with high minimum leaf size prevent overfitting on our ~1,000 game dataset.

05

Platt Scaling Calibration

Raw model probabilities are calibrated using Platt scaling — a logistic regression applied to the model's outputs. This ensures that when we say a team has a 70% chance, they actually win about 70% of the time.

06

Ensemble Output

The final prediction blends the calibrated GBC (70% weight) with a logistic regression model (30% weight). Ensembling reduces variance and improves calibration on edge cases.

Honest Limitations

What the Model Doesn't Know

NFL Oracle is a statistical model, not a crystal ball. Here's what it doesn't account for:

  • In-season injuries — the base model uses season-long averages. Use the simulator to manually adjust for known injuries.
  • Weather — the base prediction assumes neutral conditions. Use the simulator for wind/cold/rain adjustments.
  • Specific matchups — the model doesn't know that Team A's offensive line struggles against Team B's defensive scheme.
  • Motivation — Week 18 games where playoff seeds are locked, division rivalry intensity, revenge games.
  • Coaching adjustments — great coaches make halftime adjustments that statistics can't predict.
  • Randomness — even perfect predictions can't overcome turnover luck, special teams flukes, or officiating.

The model performs best on regular-season games between teams with substantial data. Early-season games (weeks 1-3) and games involving recently-changed rosters should be treated with more uncertainty.

Common Questions

FAQ

How often are predictions updated?
The model retrains every Tuesday morning with the latest play-by-play data. Team profiles update every 6 hours via the Sleeper API. Live Vegas odds update in real-time when an API key is configured.
Why does the model sometimes favor a team with a worse record?
Win-loss records are a noisy signal. A team can go 6-11 while outplaying their opponents in EPA most weeks — turnover luck, close-game losses, and schedule difficulty all distort records. EPA and Elo capture underlying performance better than wins alone.
What does "confidence" mean on each prediction?
High confidence means one team has 68%+ win probability. Moderate means 56-68%. Low means close to 50/50. These roughly correspond to the model's certainty — high confidence picks hit more often than low confidence picks.
Can I use these predictions for sports betting?
Our predictions are for entertainment only — see our Terms of Service. That said, the model's edge comes from using EPA rather than conventional stats. The model occasionally disagrees significantly with Vegas lines, which is where value betting analysis begins. Always gamble responsibly.
Why is the home team not always favored?
Home field advantage is real but modest — worth approximately 2-3 points on average. A significant talent gap easily overcomes home field. The model applies a fixed home advantage adjustment to Elo ratings, reflecting the historical ~55% home win rate in the NFL.
How is NFL Oracle different from other prediction sites?
Most public prediction tools use simple win-loss records or point differentials. NFL Oracle uses EPA — the same metric used by professional NFL analytics departments — combined with Elo for historical context, calibrated probabilities, and team-specific injury impact scaling. The What-If Simulator is unique in letting you model specific game-day scenarios.
